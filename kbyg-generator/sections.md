# KBYG Section Reference Guide

Detailed content guidance for each section of a Know Before You Go document. This file is the source of truth for what goes into each section at each tier level.

---

## Section 1: Event Overview

**Purpose:** Orient every attendee — what is this event, why are we there, and what does success look like?

### Tier 1 (Flagship)
- Event name, full dates (including move-in/move-out if applicable)
- Event description (1–2 sentences: what it is, who attends, scale)
- Audience profile: who attends this event (technical practitioners, C-suite executives, mixed), estimated attendance, area of focus
- Why we're attending — strategic context (e.g., "This is [Partner]'s flagship annual conference with 40,000+ attendees. Our presence supports our partnership goals and pipeline generation in [segment].")
- Specific goals for this event (list 2–4 measurable goals: meetings booked, leads captured, demos delivered, speaking slots, brand visibility targets)
- Our sponsorship/participation level and what it includes

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Event name, dates, venue/city
- Brief event description and audience profile (1 sentence)
- Why we're attending (1–2 sentences)
- Goals (2–3 bullet points)
- Our participation level

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Event name, dates, venue/city
- One-line description and why we're there
- Primary goal (1 bullet point)

## Section 2: Our Presence & Design Assets

**Purpose:** Help staff find us, understand the physical footprint of our participation, and know what materials and design assets are on-site.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)

**Space & Layout**
- Booth number, hall, and floor location
- Venue floor map or booth location map (embed image if provided, or link)
- Booth layout image or rendering (embed if provided — photo, floor plan, or design mockup showing the setup so staff know what to expect on arrival)
- Booth size and layout description (what to expect when you arrive)
- Meeting rooms — how many, where, how to book/check availability
- Any branded lounges, demo stations, activation spaces
- Booth hours (when we're open, when teardown begins)

**Design Assets & Materials**
- Signage and banners (description, location in booth — e.g., "2 retractable banners flanking entry, branded backdrop behind meeting table")
- Photos of design assets (embed images if provided, with captions noting what each item is and where it goes in the booth)
- Digital screens and content (what's running — demo loops, presentation decks, video reels — and where each screen is positioned)
- Printed materials (brochures, sell sheets, one-pagers, case studies — list with quantities and where they're displayed)
- Swag and giveaways (items, quantities, where they're stored at the booth, any distribution guidance — e.g., "premium swag for qualified leads only")
- Branded items (tablecloths, podium wraps, lanyards, badge holders, etc.)

This list helps staff verify setup on arrival and answer "do we have a ___?" without texting the planner.

**Demo Stations**
- What's running at each station
- Demo scripts and talking points location (link to shared folder)

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Booth/table number and location
- Meeting room info (if applicable)
- Booth hours
- Key materials on-site (signage, printed materials, swag — brief list)
- What's available at our space

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Where to find us (table, booth, or "we don't have a booth — here's where we'll gather")
- Any materials we're bringing (business cards, one-pagers, branded items)

## Section 3: Travel & Logistics

**Purpose:** Everything staff need to get there, stay there, and handle expenses correctly.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)
- Hotel name, address, booking link/code, reservation deadline
- Hotel block details (rate, check-in/check-out times, what's included)
- Airport and ground transportation guidance (recommended airlines, shuttle services, rideshare tips)
- **HR Travel Guidelines:**
  - Per diem rates and what's covered
  - Company credit card policy (what to charge, what to expense personally)
  - Travel insurance information
  - Expense reporting process and deadline (tool to use, receipt requirements, approval workflow)
- Dress code or recommended attire
- Weather/packing notes (if relevant)
- Arrival recommendations (when to arrive, when team meetings happen)

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Hotel info and booking
- Basic travel guidance
- Expense reporting process and deadline
- Per diem and credit card policy reference

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Hotel or travel basics (if overnight)
- Expense reporting reminder and deadline
- Link to full HR travel policy

**Common items people forget:** Expense reporting deadline, receipt requirements, whether meals are covered or per diem, parking info for driving events.

## Section 4: Event Calendar

**Purpose:** Show staff everything happening that involves us — and curate the broader conference agenda so they know where to be.

This is often the most important section. Organize chronologically and clearly distinguish between event types.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)

**Company-Hosted Events** (we own these)
- List each event: name, date, time, location, capacity, dress code
- Brief description and purpose
- Who's leading it
- Staff roles/assignments (if applicable)
- Registration/RSVP link (if attendees need to register)

**Company-Sponsored Events** (we sponsor but don't own)
- List each: name, date, time, location, sponsorship details
- What our sponsorship includes (branding, speaking slot, attendee list access, etc.)
- Staff responsibilities at each

**Company in the Program** (we're presenting, on a panel, moderating, etc.)
- List each: session name, date, time, room
- Speaker(s) and their topic
- How other staff can attend/support

**Highlighted Events to Attend**

Beyond our own events, curate the conference agenda for the team. These are sessions and events staff should prioritize attending even though we're not hosting or sponsoring them.

- **Partner Events:** Events hosted by our partners where we should have presence, show support, or network. Note why each matters and who from our team should attend.
- **Industry-Relevant Sessions:** Sessions tied to our market, competitive landscape, technology trends, or product positioning. Note what to listen for.
- **C-Suite / Executive Events:** Events where senior executives (ours, partners', or prospects') will be present — important for staff looking to make introductions or secure meetings.

For each highlighted event, include: session name, date/time, location, and a brief note on why it's important and who should prioritize attending.

**How to Register/Participate**
- Links to register for sessions, reserve seats, sign up for booth shifts
- Deadlines for registration
- How to add events to your personal calendar

**Run of Show**
- How staff can track their personal schedule and assigned participation
- Tool or document where the master schedule lives
- Where to check for updates during the event

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Hosted events listed with date/time/location
- Program sessions with speakers
- Key highlighted events (partner + industry, 3–5 picks with brief notes)
- Registration links
- Brief run of show or schedule reference

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Key sessions to attend (just the list with times)
- Any company-led sessions
- Link to event agenda

## Section 5: Company Updates & Talking Points

**Purpose:** Arm every attendee with current, accurate information about the company so they represent us well.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)
- **Product Updates:** Recent launches, features, announcements. What's new since the last major event. What we're announcing at this event (if applicable).
- **Department Updates:** Any org changes, new hires, team news that's relevant to mention externally.
- **Key Talking Points:** 3–5 bullet points that every staff member should be able to speak to naturally. These aren't scripts — they're themes. (e.g., "We just launched [feature] which helps [audience] do [outcome].")
- **Highlights:** Awards, press mentions, partnerships, customer wins — things staff can reference in conversation.
- **What NOT to discuss:** Any embargoed info, upcoming unannounced products, or sensitive topics with guidance on how to deflect gracefully.

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Key product updates (2–3 bullets)
- Top talking points (2–3 bullets)
- Notable highlights to reference

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- One-paragraph company update
- 1–2 talking points

## Section 6: Event Tech & Lead Qualification

**Purpose:** Make sure staff can use the tools AND understand how to qualify leads effectively — not just scan badges.

This section goes beyond "here's the app" into practical training on what makes a lead worth pursuing and how to capture the information that helps sales follow up successfully.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)

**Lead Capture Tool**
- Which tool (Captello, Popl, badge scanner, etc.), how to access it, quick-start instructions
- Download and log in BEFORE you arrive — link and credentials
- What information to capture per lead: name, company, title, interest area, conversation notes, follow-up priority

**Lead Qualification — MQL vs SQL**
- **What is an MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead)?** Someone who shows interest and fits the target profile but hasn't demonstrated specific buying intent. Examples: visited the booth, attended a session, asked general questions, picked up materials.
- **What is an SQL (Sales Qualified Lead)?** Someone with a specific need, timeline, budget, or decision-making authority. Examples: asked about pricing, described a problem our product solves, requested a follow-up meeting, mentioned an active evaluation.
- **How to classify during a conversation:**
  - Ask: "What brought you to our booth?" (gauges intent)
  - Ask: "Are you currently evaluating solutions in this space?" (gauges timeline)
  - Ask: "What's your role in this decision?" (gauges authority)
  - If they have need + timeline + authority → SQL. Tag accordingly.
  - If they're interested but early-stage → MQL. Still valuable — capture good notes.
- **What notes to capture that actually help follow-up:** Don't just write "seemed interested." Write what they're interested IN, what problem they described, what they're currently using, and what they asked about. The follow-up person should be able to pick up the conversation where you left off.
- **Lead scoring guidance:** Hot (request meeting / active evaluation) · Warm (interested, good fit, no immediate timeline) · Cool (general interest, informational)

**Event App**
- Name, download link, how to use it for scheduling and navigation

**Badge Scanning**
- How it works, what data it captures, any special instructions

**Meeting Scheduling Tool**
- How to view/book/manage meetings on-site

**Internal Comms**
- Which Slack channel, group chat, or tool to use for real-time coordination during the event
- Keep notifications on during event hours

**WiFi**
- Network name and password (if known)

**If tech breaks on-site:** Who to contact and where to find them

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Lead capture tool and basic instructions
- Brief MQL vs SQL definitions with 1–2 example questions to ask
- Lead scoring: hot / warm / cool with brief descriptions
- Event app download link
- Internal comms channel for the event

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Lead capture method (even if it's "collect business cards and photograph them")
- One-line reminder: capture name, company, what they're interested in, and follow-up priority
- Internal comms channel

**Common items people forget:** Login credentials distributed before the event, lead capture app tested before arriving, internal Slack channel created in advance, staff not knowing what makes a lead worth escalating to sales.

## Section 7: Eyes & Ears Guide

**Purpose:** Turn every attendee into an intelligence collector. Staff are your eyes on the ground at events you can't fully attend yourself.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)

**Photo & Video Collection**
- What to capture: our booth (with and without crowds), our speakers on stage, team moments, notable competitor booths, creative activations, swag ideas, signage/branding you admire
- How to share: designated Slack channel, shared album link, or upload folder
- Tips: take wide shots AND detail shots, capture booth numbers so we can reference later, avoid photographing people without their awareness in close-up

**Session Assessment**
- Which sessions to prioritize attending (linked to company interests)
- What to listen for: competitor announcements, market trends, customer pain points, partnership signals
- How to report: brief notes format — session name, speaker, 3 key takeaways, any action items for us

**Competitive & Market Intelligence**
- What to look for at other booths: messaging themes, demo approaches, booth traffic levels, staffing models, creative giveaways, interactive elements
- Note any new competitors or unexpected players
- Capture pricing or positioning language if visible

**Questions to Ask**
- Suggested questions for conversations with other attendees, partners, and prospects (tailored to event goals)
- What to listen for in hallway conversations and networking events

**How to Report Back**
- Where to submit observations (Slack channel, shared doc, post-event debrief form)
- Deadline for submitting notes

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Photo guidance (what to capture, where to share)
- Key things to look for at other booths and sessions
- How to share observations

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Brief reminder: capture photos of our presence, anything interesting from other exhibitors
- Share to [channel/folder]

## Section 8: Post-Event Reporting

**Purpose:** Set expectations BEFORE the event so people capture what's needed during.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)
- What's expected from each attendee after the event (individual debrief notes, lead follow-up, session takeaways)
- Deadline for post-event submissions
- Format and where to submit (form, shared doc, Slack thread, etc.)
- Metrics each person should track: meetings held, leads captured, demos given, key conversations, competitive intel gathered
- Post-event debrief meeting: when it's scheduled, what to prepare
- Lead follow-up expectations: timeline for follow-up, who owns which leads, handoff process to sales

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Reporting expectations and deadline
- Key metrics to track
- Lead follow-up process

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Brief reminder to submit notes and leads by [date]
- Link to reporting form or channel

**Common items people forget:** Setting the debrief meeting date before the event, establishing lead follow-up ownership before the event (not after).

## Section 9: Staff Attending

**Purpose:** Every attendee knows who else is there, what they bring, and how to reach them.

At any event with multiple staff attending, people need to know who's on the ground and who to tap for specific situations. If someone at the booth gets a deep technical question, they need to know who can help. If someone meets a VP who wants an executive conversation, they need to know who to introduce.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)

**Staff Roster**

| Name | Role / Title | Expertise Area | Professional Contact | Booth Shifts |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Name] | [Title] | [e.g., Product/API, Enterprise Sales, Partnerships] | [work phone, work email] | [shift times] |

- **Who to pull in for specific conversations:**
  - Technical / product deep-dive questions → [name(s)]
  - Partnership or business development discussions → [name(s)]
  - Executive / C-suite meetings → [name(s)]
  - Press or analyst inquiries → [name(s)]
  - Pricing or deal structure → [name(s)]

- List only professional contact info (work phone, work email) — never personal

### Tier 2 (Significant)

**Staff Roster**

| Name | Role / Title | Professional Contact |
|---|---|---|
| [Name] | [Title] | [work phone, work email] |

- Brief note on who handles what if questions come up on-site

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- List of who's attending (names and roles)
- Event planner contact for any questions

## Section 10: Key Contacts

**Purpose:** Everyone knows exactly who to reach when something comes up.

This section adds contacts that are NOT in the Staff Attending section — primarily the event planner, vendors, and emergency contacts. For Tier 3 events with very small teams, Sections 9 and 10 can be combined into a single "People & Contacts" section to avoid repetition.

### Tier 1 (Flagship)
- **Event Planner(s):** Name, phone, email — the person running logistics
- **Staff Leading Specific Events:** Who's responsible for each hosted event, dinner, demo, etc.
- **Other Staff With Event Roles:** Booth shift leads, speaker liaisons, meeting coordinators
- **On-Site Emergency Contacts:** Venue security, medical, local emergency numbers
- **Vendor/Partner Contacts:** AV provider, catering, booth builder — anyone staff might need to reach on-site
- **Internal Emergency Contact:** Who to call if something goes seriously wrong (e.g., manager, ops lead)

### Tier 2 (Significant)
- Event planner + phone
- Key staff leads
- Emergency contacts

### Tier 3 (Small/Regional)
- Event planner contact
- Emergency contact

## Formatting & Style Notes

- **Keep it scannable, not short.** Length follows the event — a flagship Tier 1 with full calendars, a design-asset inventory, and a large roster will run long, and that's correct. "Light" means every line earns its place: bullets over paragraphs, tables over prose, no filler. A Tier 3 is brief because the *event* is small, not because detail was cut.
- **Use bullet points aggressively.** No one reads paragraphs in a KBYG.
- **Bold key info:** dates, deadlines, locations, names.
- **Link everything.** Registration links, hotel booking, shared folders, event app downloads — make them clickable.
- **Include visual breaks.** Use section headers and horizontal rules to make scanning easy.
- **Embed images** where provided: booth layout, floor maps, design asset photos. Add brief captions noting what each image shows and where items are placed.
- **Version and date the document.** Staff need to know they have the latest version.
- **Put the most time-sensitive info first** within each section.

## Scope Note

This skill generates **pre-event** briefing documents. If the user asks for post-event documentation, reporting templates, or retrospective analysis, direct them to the master event schema (Section C: Post-Event Actuals) or a post-event report workflow. The KBYG is not a post-event tool.
